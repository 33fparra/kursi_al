The history of a country begins long before it ever gets its name.

If you’re from the West, you probably know this country as Albania. I did too. For most of my life, I called it "Albania," spoke of "Albanians," and assumed that was how the people living here identified themselves.

Until I moved here.

That was when I discovered something that, while seemingly small, completely changes how you understand this story: they almost never say "Albania." They say **Shqipëri**. They rarely call themselves "Albanians"—they say **Shqiptarë**. And their language isn't "Albanian"; it's **Shqip**.

It might seem like a minor detail. But back when I lived outside my home country, Chile, it always bothered me whenever English speakers mispronounced it as *“Killi.”* It felt jarring and unnatural. Since my personal philosophy is to treat others the way I’d like to be treated, I prefer using the names people are actually comfortable with. Out of respect for how these people identify themselves, throughout this series I will use—whenever possible—the names used by the inhabitants themselves: **Shqipëri**, **Shqiptarë**, and **Shqip**.

That said, a quick disclaimer is necessary: when we turn the clock back thousands of years, even those names didn't exist yet. And that is precisely the first surprise of this story.

Because before the Shqiptarë...  
Before the Illyrians...  
Before Rome...  
Before Greece...  

People lived here for thousands of years. We know how they built their homes, what crops they grew, and who they traded with. But there is one thing we can never recover: we don't know a single word of the language they spoke. Not one.

## A Balkan Story that Began Long Before Written History

When we think of prehistory, we usually picture people chasing mammoths with stone spears or fleeing saber-toothed tigers. Yet thousands of years ago, the land that is now Shqipëri was already undergoing a revolution. Not technological, not political, but something far more fundamental: **the revolution of learning to stay**.

For thousands of generations, human beings had followed the animals and the seasons—in other words, they were nomads. Now, they were beginning to do the exact opposite:

- They planted.
- They waited.
- They harvested.
- They built permanent homes.
- They raised sheep, goats, and cattle.

For the first time, people could imagine their children growing up in the exact same spot where their parents were born. They became settled.

Today, this feels normal—in fact, most of us live this way—but back then, it was a revolutionary concept. It transformed the economy, diet, family structure, land ownership, and very likely the way people thought.

## A Balkan People Far More Connected Than We Imagine

There’s another myth about prehistory: that these early villages were completely isolated. Archaeological discoveries tell a very different story.

Across various Balkan settlements, artifacts made of obsidian from volcanic regions hundreds of miles away keep turning up. Archaeologists also find jewelry crafted from *Spondylus* sea shells originating in the Aegean Sea.

No single trader walked a thousand miles just to sell a seashell. Instead, those shells traveled vast distances by passing from one community to the next, like a massive commercial domino effect. Long before coins existed, trade networks were already thriving.

And it wasn't just objects traveling—ideas traveled too: new techniques for crafting pottery, new tools, better agricultural methods, and perhaps even new spiritual beliefs. The Balkans were already a meeting place between different worlds.

## The Language Lost to Time

Imagine if all literature in English or Spanish vanished overnight—no books, no inscriptions, no letters, no digital messages. Five thousand years from now, someone might uncover our cities, highways, and buildings, but they would never know what our language sounded like.

That is exactly what happened here. Archaeologists can reconstruct how these people lived, but writing hadn't reached this region yet. And when a language vanishes before being written down, it vanishes forever.

Thousands of generations were born speaking those tongues. They fell in love, argued, sang, cried, raised children, and shared stories around the fire... and all of it was lost without leaving a single sentence behind. It is perhaps one of the greatest silences in all of European history.

## Were These the First Inhabitants of Albania (Shqiptarë)?

It’s an inevitable question, and the most honest answer is: **we don't know**. In fact, the question itself is probably misframed.

They weren't Shqiptarë, nor were they Illyrians, Greeks, Romans, or Slavs. All of those identities emerged thousands of years later. Talking about "Albanians" eight thousand years ago would be like saying Adam and Eve were Iraqi, or that the tribes living in Oceania 4,000 years ago called themselves "Australians." It wouldn't make much sense, would it?

There is geographic continuity, but not necessarily cultural, linguistic, or political continuity. What we *do* know is that these communities represent the first major known chapter of human history on this land. They aren't the proven origin of the modern Shqiptar people, but they are the known origin of continuous human settlement here. And that distinction matters, because history is rarely a straight line—it is a long series of encounters, mixtures, and transformations.

## The Arrival of New Peoples from the Steppes

For nearly three thousand years, these farming communities thrived. But Europe never stood still. Around 3000 BCE, a transformation began that would reshape the continent forever.

From the vast steppes north of the Black Sea, human groups with a drastically different way of life began to spread. They were far more mobile, heavily reliant on herding, and—as extensive genetic research reveals—carried a significant portion of the ancestry shared by many European populations today.

What’s fascinating is that current evidence doesn't point to a simple population replacement, but rather an integration:

1. The farmers who had lived in the Balkans for millennia didn't disappear overnight.
2. They encountered the newcomers and coexisted.
3. They exchanged knowledge and intermarried.
4. Gradually, new communities emerged.

To what extent this mixture gave rise to the peoples later known as the **Illyrians** remains an active field of research. Each new ancient DNA study adds another piece to the puzzle, though many questions remain unanswered.

And perhaps that is the most important takeaway from this first chapter: identity doesn't spring from a single isolated group—it emerges from the meeting of many. This completely dismantles extreme concepts like the Nazi myth of an "Aryan race"; biologically, it is baseless, as even the most isolated groups are the product of centuries of human blending.

## The True Beginning

When we think of the history of Albania or Shqipëri, we tend to look for heroes, kings, epic battles, and empires. But the true beginning has no famous names—it has farmers, shepherds, and artisans. We don't even know what they called themselves or what their language sounded like.

Yet they were the ones who began transforming this corner of the Balkans into a permanently settled home. The history of the Shqiptarë doesn't strictly start with them, but without them, the history that followed wouldn't have been possible either.

In the next chapter, we'll meet the newcomers from the vast Eurasian steppes. They didn't arrive to erase the past, but to blend with it. And within that blend, a crucial piece of the human mosaic began to take shape—one that, centuries later, would give rise to the peoples classical sources would call the **Illyrians**.
