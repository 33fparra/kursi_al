## My Mother’s Jewelry Box and Myths About Ancient Albania

A few days ago, I opened my mother's jewelry box. Before I even looked inside, my mind had already constructed an image. When we think of a jewelry box—especially through the lens of movies, novels, and fairy tales—we usually imagine something golden: a small chest filled with gold, sparkling gemstones, and glittering treasures waiting to be discovered.

That was the picture in my head. But when I opened it, something curious happened: there was virtually no gold at all. Everything was silver.

At first, I was surprised, until I remembered a simple fact: my mother never liked gold. She always preferred silver. The issue wasn't with the jewelry box; the issue was the story I had created in my mind before ever opening it.

We often do the exact same thing when we look at the past. We construct a narrative before we ever look at the evidence.

When we think of prehistoric societies—especially those that existed thousands of years ago in the Balkans and the territory of modern-day Albania—we tend to picture small, isolated groups. People living their entire lives in one spot, communities separated by impenetrable mountains and dense forests with virtually no contact with the outside world.

Yet when we begin examining the clues they left behind, a strikingly different reality emerges.

## A Balkan Tomb with an Awkward Question: The Spondylus Shell

Imagine opening and stepping inside a 6,000-year-old tomb deep in the Balkan interior. It doesn't belong to a king. There is no grand monument. It is the grave of an ordinary person, meaning there are no written inscriptions detailing their life. All that remains are a few grave goods: stone tools, pottery fragments, and personal items buried alongside the deceased.

Among them sits a single seashell. At first glance, it seems like a minor detail. But if you think deeper, it raises a massive question: this community was located far from the sea. Very far.

The shell belongs to the genus *Spondylus*, a type of spiny oyster found primarily in the Eastern Mediterranean, particularly the Aegean Sea. So how on earth did it get here?

Our modern instinct is to imagine a grand adventure—a single traveler trekking hundreds of miles across rugged mountain ranges with a bag of precious goods, like a character straight out of *The Lord of the Rings*.

In reality, what happened was likely much simpler and far more fascinating.

No single person needed to make the entire journey; the shell could have passed through dozens of hands. A coastal community likely harvested it first. Then, a neighboring village acquired it through trade. Sometime later, it passed to a third community. Perhaps a family kept it for generations. Eventually, it ended up buried alongside someone who had never set foot anywhere near the coast. The person in that grave may have never seen the ocean, yet they cherished that shell as a priceless treasure.

The movement wasn't in a single hero. It was in the network.

## Trade Before Money: Prehistoric Exchange Networks in Albania

When we think of commerce today, we think of retail stores, set prices, currency, or digital payments like PayPal and Stripe. But for the vast majority of human history, none of that existed.

Imagine you needed meat, but your family grew carrots and tomatoes. The neighboring village hunted rabbits. When you wanted rabbit for dinner, you traded five carrots and three tomatoes for one. Sounds rudimentary, doesn't it? But it was a rudimentary time.

Communities exchanged what they had for what others needed. One region might possess superior agricultural soil; another had access to high-quality flint; another held mineral deposits. Coastal groups had marine items unavailable inland, while interior groups held resources highly valued along the coast.

The *Spondylus* shell wasn't valuable because it could feed a family or serve as a practical tool. It was valuable because it represented something rare, exotic, and brought from afar. In a world where distance was vastly harder to overcome than it is today, that exotic origin carried immense symbolic prestige.

## Objects Were Only the Visible Part: Ideas and Craftsmanship

What is most fascinating is that objects weren't the only things traveling. Imagine an artisan from a small Balkan village watching a craftsperson from another region use a technique he’s never seen before. He has no textbook, no video tutorial, and no written language. He only has the person standing in front of him. So he watches, asks questions, imitates, and tests the technique himself.

This was how humanity's greatest innovations spread for thousands of years. Techniques traveled because people traveled. A new method for firing pottery, knapping stone, or working raw metal could cross vast continents without a single word ever being written down.

## The Adriatic Sea: Not a Border, But a Highway

Because of this constant movement, the Adriatic Sea was never a barrier. Today, when we look at a map, we see nation-states separated by water: Italy, Croatia, Montenegro, Albania, and Greece. But those divisions are modern concepts. For ancient communities, the Adriatic was a highway.

Balkan geography features a distinct trait: towering mountain ranges create formidable land barriers. In many cases, hugging the coastline in small vessels was far easier than trekking across rugged interior mountains. We aren't talking about massive merchant fleets, but rather small-scale coastal trips hopping from bay to bay along known routes. Repeated over generations, those short journeys forged enduring regional connections.

## The True Silent Protagonist: Water and the Bridge to Vučedol

When discussing these early societies, we must remain careful. At this stage in history, neither the Shqiptarë nor the Illyrians existed as recognized historical identities. Labeling these prehistoric populations as "Albanians" would be anachronistic. The accurate approach is to recognize that we are viewing the foundational stage where, over time, new cultures would eventually crystallize.

Yet if there was one true silent protagonist throughout this entire saga, it wasn't the seashell. It was water.

Until now, we've viewed water as a transport route, but long before it became a commercial highway, water was a matter of basic survival. A human being can survive weeks without food, but only a few days without water. Early communities didn't settle randomly; they sought rivers, lakes, and natural springs capable of sustaining life year-round.

First, water allowed people to settle in one place. Then, it made agriculture possible. Over time, those same rivers linked neighboring villages. And once humans mastered navigation, seas transformed from intimidating obstacles into open roads. It is remarkable to reflect on how the very element that quenched human thirst ultimately connected entire civilizations.

If we step back and observe history from a distance, a clear pattern emerges: where there is water, life takes root; where life remains, exchange flourishes; and where exchange thrives over centuries, new cultures inevitably rise.

That is precisely what we will explore in the next chapter. Over time, the very networks that once traded shells and stone-knapping techniques were about to carry something far more powerful: the secret of turning raw rock into molten metal. On the banks of a major river, a community was about to master that secret—and discover that power, this time, came with a cost no one had foreseen. Archaeologists know them as the **Vučedol culture**.
