## When the Sea Was Faster Than the Land

If you want to travel from one city to another today, you probably open Google Maps. Your phone calculates the fastest route and almost certainly sends you along a paved highway. Our entire mindset is shaped by the modern world: when we think of travel, we automatically think of land. But four thousand years ago, the exact opposite was true.

Imagine living along the eastern coast of the Adriatic. In front of you lies the open sea; behind you rise some of the most formidable, impassable mountain ranges in Europe. Which route would you choose? Would you trek for days through dense forests, rocky peaks, and roaring rivers... or step into a small boat and glide along the coastline, anchoring each night in a calm, sheltered bay?

To us today, the sea looks like a barrier. To those ancient communities, it was a road. Or, more accurately, a highway.

## The Cetina Culture: A Society That Understood the Map

Around 2200 BCE, a culture emerged that leveraged this maritime advantage far better than many of its neighbors. Archaeologists know them as the **Cetina culture**. The name doesn't come from an ancient king or a self-proclaimed tribe; it follows a standard archaeological convention where lost cultures without written records are named after the site where their material remains were first identified. In this case, that site was the Cetina River valley in modern-day Croatia.

Those people almost certainly never called themselves "Cetinas"; we simply do not know their true name. But we do know something far more important: we know where they lived and how they moved.

When you look at a map, their choice seems almost inevitable. The Cetina Valley acts as a natural corridor linking the Balkan interior directly to the Adriatic coast. From there, one could set sail north, head south, or even cross over to the Italian peninsula. It wasn't just any spot on the coast—it was the ultimate springboard.

## Navigation as Survival in the Ancient Balkans

We sometimes picture ancient seafaring as heroic, lone expeditions battling the open ocean for weeks on end, much like Tom Hanks in the movie *Cast Away*. In reality, ancient seafaring was far less Hollywood and far more pragmatic.

Most journeys didn't involve crossing the open Adriatic from shoreline to shoreline. Instead, sailors hugged the coast—taking advantage of good weather, seeking shelter the moment winds shifted, and making frequent stops. It was a step-by-step journey where every known bay reduced risk, and every coastal village offered a place to repair a vessel, exchange news, or replenish supplies. Over generations, these repeated coastal routes developed into genuine communication networks.

## Why Risk Your Life? Prestige Over Money

Still, an inevitable question arises: why do it? Seafaring four thousand years ago was undeniably dangerous. There were no compasses, engines, or lighthouses, and early boats were hardly seaworthy fortresses—a sudden storm could end everything in minutes. What motivated someone to take such immense risks?

Our modern answer is usually money, but currency did not exist yet. Back then, true wealth was measured differently: through **prestige**.

Imagine a small village where every family has crafted the exact same style of pottery for generations. One day, a sailor arrives carrying a vessel no one has ever seen. It has a different shape, a unique color, and an unfamiliar firing technique. Everyone wants to touch it; everyone asks where it came from. Suddenly, that sailor hasn't just brought an object—he has brought a story.

For much of human history, stories conferred immense social status. Traveling meant venturing into the unknown, forging alliances with distant communities, and returning with exotic goods no one else could obtain. That transformed the traveler into a respected, influential figure within his community.

## The Coast of Albania: A Strategic Hub on Maritime Routes

Archaeologists identify the Cetina culture primarily through a distinct style of decorated pottery found scattered across numerous Adriatic sites. But pottery is merely the tangible footprint of a deeper phenomenon: it proves there was constant contact between communities separated by hundreds of miles.

And wherever objects travel, people travel too. With them travel new tools, innovative agricultural practices, novel building techniques, and new family ties. Far from dividing the region's cultures, the Adriatic connected them.

The coastline of modern-day Shqipëri (Albania) became an active participant in this maritime web. Coastal settlements and archaeological finds reveal that this territory was no longer an isolated pocket of the Balkan map, but a strategic, natural stopover for sailors navigating the sea—a place to rest, trade goods, and resume the journey.

Without realizing it, those early communities were turning this coastline into coveted territory that would retain its strategic value for millennia. Centuries later, Greeks, Romans, Venetians, and Ottomans would all recognize the vital importance of controlling these exact same routes.

## When Wealth Appears, a New Challenge Begins

For thousands of years, villages had focused on producing food to survive. Now, they were moving goods, technologies, and specialized knowledge over ever-greater distances. This brought unprecedented opportunities, but it also meant the accumulation of valuable wealth.

And history teaches us that whenever a society accumulates wealth, an inevitable question soon follows: *How do we protect it?*

In the next chapter, we will see how certain communities stopped being open, peaceful villages and began reorganizing. The first hillforts and fortifications emerge, the power of warrior elites grows, and social standing is no longer measured solely by the land you till—it begins to be measured by what you are capable of defending.
