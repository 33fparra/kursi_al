Every time a government announces an increase in the minimum wage, the exact same scene repeats itself.

Headlines celebrate.

Social networks applaud.

Politicians smile in front of the cameras.

And most people think the exact same thing:

"Finally, someone is helping the workers."

And, to be honest, they are often right.

No one should work forty hours a week and still struggle to pay for grocery shopping or the electricity bill.

A dignified minimum wage is a vital social milestone.

The problem is that the real economy rarely works solely on good intentions.

It works on numbers.

And sometimes numbers have the unpleasant habit of disobeying political speeches.

---

## Forget About Big Millionaires

It is very difficult to ask for empathy toward a wealthy business tycoon from someone who is counting coins just to make ends meet.

So let’s forget about large corporations for a moment.

Let’s look at something much closer to home.

Think about your uncle who runs a small car repair shop.

Think about your grandfather who owns a neighborhood bakery.

Think about your brother who opened a small retail shop after years of hard-earned savings.

They do not own private jets.

They do not have millionaire accounts in Switzerland.

They open their shops before the sun rises.

They pay commercial rent.

They pay electricity bills under business tariffs.

They pay taxes.

And some months they hit day 30 wondering if they will manage to make it to day 31.

This is the living reality for thousands of small and medium-sized enterprises across Kosovo and Albania.

---

## The Illusion of the Round Number

Most people assume something very straightforward: if the minimum wage is 500 euros, then the employer pays 500 euros and the worker receives 500 euros.

It sounds logical.

But it is simply not true.

Between the cash leaving the business register and the money landing in the worker’s bank account, there is a much longer path than it seems.

Let's take the example of Kosovo in 2026.

The gross minimum wage is set at 500 euros.

The worker, after Trust and TAP deductions, receives approximately **456.50 euros net**.

But the employer does not just pay 500 euros either. The company must also chip in a mandatory **5% contribution** out of pocket to the worker's pension fund. That means an additional 25 euros.

Therefore:

* **The minimum cost for the company:** approximately 525 euros.
* **The money the worker actually receives:** approximately 456.50 euros.

Between those two figures lies a gap of nearly **70 euros**. And we are not done yet.

## The Invisible Employee Who Also Collects a Check Each Month

There is another worker in the business that almost nobody talks about on television: **the accountant**.

Your grandfather might bake the best bread in town. Your uncle might fix an engine with his eyes closed. But chances are, neither of them knows how to file tax returns, manage employment contracts, or process payroll according to current labor laws.

That is why they need professional help. And that help costs money every single month.

Then come the other overheads:

* Mandatory occupational health checkups;
* Labor code inspections and compliance certificates;
* Risk prevention programs;
* Banking fees for salary wire transfers;
* Management and invoicing software;
* Mandatory insurance policies.

Suddenly, that minimum-wage employee no longer costs the business 500 euros. They cost significantly more. Far more than most people imagine.

---

## The Problem Appears When the Math Stops Balancing Out

Now imagine your grandfather runs a small bakery with four workers. Operating margins are tight, but the business works.

Suddenly, a sharp, sudden hike in the minimum wage is mandated. Let's move from the current example of 500 euros to a hypothetical gross increase up to 650 euros.

Each worker now costs far more. And that increase doesn't happen just once. It is multiplied by four workers. Every month. Every year.

What would you do?

* Would you shut down the business?
* Would you work for free?
* Would you take out bank loans indefinitely just to cover payroll?

Most small businesses make a much simpler and far more painful choice: **they downsize**. Four workers become three.

Not because the employer is malicious. Not because they hate their staff. Simply because the cash register has physical limits that no government decree can alter.

---

## What Economists Actually Say

It is crucial to be precise here. International economic research does not state that raising the minimum wage automatically destroys jobs. Reality is far more nuanced.

In some sectors, the impact is practically non-existent. In others, it can even boost productivity and reduce employee turnover.

But there is one thing on which a broad consensus exists: **when wage increases are too rapid and are not backed by gains in productivity, small businesses suffer the heaviest structural strain.**

Especially those operating on razor-thin profit margins. A large multinational corporation can absorb these shocks. The neighborhood bakery usually cannot.

---

## What Is the Real Solution?

The answer is likely not choosing between workers and business owners. Because both need each other to exist. The true solution generally rests on three core pillars:

1. **Gradual and Predictable Increases:** Businesses can adjust to changes when hikes are announced well in advance. What destroys planning are abrupt, politically driven updates.
2. **Lifting the Burden Off Job Creators:** If the state desires higher wages, it can also slash some of the administrative and tax weights born by small enterprises. That way, the money reaches the worker without suffocating the provider of the job.
3. **Driving Productivity:** This is the most critical point of all. Sustainable wages are not born in a government decree. They are born when the mechanic fixes more cars. When the bakery sells more bread. When the business generates more intrinsic value. Technology and professional training are the real engines.

The correct question is not how much a worker should earn. The correct question is: **How do we enable small companies to pay higher wages without vanishing along the way?**

Because a spectacular minimum wage written into a paper law is of very little use if six months later the shop where you worked is forced to lock its doors forever.

## ❓ Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. Why are small businesses more vulnerable to minimum wage hikes than large corporations?**
Large corporations benefit from higher profit margins, economies of scale, and the capability to automate processes. A small family business relies heavily on manual labor and carries fixed operational costs that cannot be altered overnight.

**2. Does a sudden minimum wage increase drive inflation?**
If wages are pushed up solely by decree without a matching increase in production, small businesses are forced to raise the prices of their goods to cover the new labor costs. This drives up the baseline cost of living, quickly neutralizing the initial wage gains.

**3. How can the state support SMEs during these wage transitions?**
The state can introduce fiscal relief, such as temporarily subsidizing pension or social security contributions for minimum-wage employees, or cutting commercial license fees and baseline local small-business taxes.
