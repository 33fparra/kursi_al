When I was a child, my dad used to tell me that if I wanted permission to go out on weekends, I had to maintain a school grade average above 9.0. If my grade was even an 8.9, there was absolutely no chance of getting permission. There were rules, a clear limit, and zero room for negotiation.

Nowadays, something very similar happens to us, but in a completely different arena.

Anyone who has gone out looking for a two-bedroom (2+1) apartment in Tirana recently knows very well that real estate market prices have undergone an increase that is, let's face it, exaggerated. What cost €80,000 a few years ago is now hard to find for less than €120,000 in expanding areas like Astir or Yzberisht. If your goal is a more consolidated neighborhood like Komuna e Parisit, Don Bosko, or Rruga e Elbasanit, the figures easily surpass €170,000, according to the latest housing trend reports in the capital.

Faced with this scenario, the idea of buying a home by saving your money under the mattress and paying in cash has turned into an impossible dream for anyone belonging to the middle class. The only real alternative left is to turn to a bank or financial institution to request a **mortgage**.

But, before evaluating your savings history or property collateral, exactly like when I asked my dad for permission and he asked me *"what grade did you get?"*, now, 15 years later, when we ask the bank for financing, it asks us: *"How much is your salary?"*. And depending on our answer, it will determine whether they serve us with a smile or if they laugh at us before we walk out the door.

In Albania, the answer to that specific question is the exact point where most applications crash against the reality of the regulatory system.

## The Golden Rule: The certified debt-to-income ratio

Commercial banks in Albania do not calculate the viability of a loan based on informal promises or projections. All institutions operate under the strict guidelines of the *Regulation on Credit Risk Management* drafted by the [Bank of Albania](https://www.bankofalbania.org/Supervision/Regulations/). This regulation requires the rigorous application of what is known as the **Debt-to-Income ratio (DTI)** (*Koeficienti i borxhit*).

The general rule of the Albanian financial market for this year 2026 defines a very clear limit:

> **The monthly loan payment (kësti) cannot exceed 30% or a maximum of 40% of the applicant's certified and provable official net income.**

In simple words: if you receive your money officially through the bank and have an income of €100, the bank will never allow you to have a monthly payment higher than €40.

The remaining 60% of your official salary must remain completely free. The bank, according to consumer protection regulations, assumes that this percentage is the minimum vital buffer you need to cover food, basic utility bills, transportation, and to maintain a dignified lifestyle in a Tirana where the cost of living rises every day. Therefore, if your official salary registered in your bank account is **80,000 Lekë** (a figure roughly considered the average salary in our country), your maximum allowed monthly payment will never exceed 32,000 Lekë per month.

## The real math for a standard apartment in 2026

To understand how this regulation translates into your daily routine, let's analyze the performance of a standard home loan in euros, using current average market interest rates (which float between 4% and 5.5% annually).

If we take a repayment term of 20 years as a reference and the mandatory bank condition to pay a **20% down payment (paradhënia)** out of your own pocket, the required official salary scenario breaks down as follows:

| Property Price | Down Payment (20%) | Bank Loan | Monthly Payment (*Kësti*) | Minimum Official Net Salary |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **€100.000** | €20.000 | €80.000 | ~€506 (47,700 Lekë) | **119,250 Lekë** |
| **€130.000** | €26.000 | €104.000 | ~€650 (61,300 Lekë) | **153,250 Lekë** |
| **€160.000** | €32.000 | €128.000 | ~€800 (75,500 Lekë) | **188,750 Lekë** |

<br>

As clearly shown by the table data, to purchase an average apartment worth €130,000, the bank will require you to verify a net family income of at least 153,250 Lekë per month (a figure that already represents the upper-middle class in the country).

Before starting procedures or submitting documents, you can [check your loan qualification on leku.al](https://leku.al/albania/kualifikim/), where you can simulate your real monthly payment and factor in the **NEI (Effective Interest Rate)** to see hidden costs.

## The cultural brake: "Bashkë-kredimarrës" and the weight of the envelope

When you compare these requirements with our job market, the gap is alarming. According to official wage data published by [INSTAT](https://www.instat.gov.al/), the average wage in the formal private sector is far below the levels required to buy a home alone in the capital. It's pretty much like wanting to become a professional basketball player but your height is 1.50 meters; it looks like an extremely difficult mission.

To overcome this financial wall, culture and the banking system in Albania massively resort to two workarounds:

### 1. The Co-borrower (Bashkë-kredimarrës)
Since a single average salary cannot qualify for current property prices in Tirana, the vast majority of mortgages are signed by joining the official income of the couple (husband and wife) or adding the payrolls of parents and children. By merging two salaries into a single block (for example, one of 80,000 Lekë and another of 75,000 Lekë), the family reaches the target block of 155,000 Lekë net, getting the green light from the bank's risk department. This is the most widely used technique today.

### 2. The critical impact of the "Paga në zarf" (Salary in envelopes)
Partial job informality is the biggest enemy of homeownership in Albania. If a qualified professional receives a real salary of 130,000 Lekë, but their employer has registered them in the contract under the legal minimum wage of 50,000 Lekë (giving the rest cash in an envelope to avoid taxes), **for the banking system that employee only earns 50,000 Lekë**.

Bank analysts rely solely on the official historical statements that appear in the system of the [General Directorate of Taxes](https://www.tatime.gov.al/). Undeclared money, no matter how real and tangible it is every month-end in your pocket, does not exist when evaluating your repayment capacity for a home loan.

In conclusion, before entering into negotiations or signing off-plan orders with construction firms, the primary step is to check the health of your official payroll. Your purchasing power in Tirana is not determined by the total money brought home, but by the money that the state and the bank can legally verify.
