There is a notification that thousands of workers in Kosovo wait for every month with almost the same level of excitement.

"Your salary has been processed."

650 euros.

700 euros.

Perhaps a little more if the month was good.

For a few seconds, that feeling of relief we all know appears:

"The salary is here."

But then you open the bank app.

And something strange happens.

The salary is there.

And at the same time, it isn't.

You expected to see 650 euros available.

On the screen, 40 euros appear.

Perhaps 20.

Perhaps simply a flat zero.

The rest disappeared in a matter of seconds.

There were no purchases.

There were no withdrawals.

There was no mistake.

The bank simply automatically collected the money it had advanced you the previous month.

Welcome to the world of **minus i lejuar**, the "allowed minus."

A financial product so common in Kosovo that many people have even stopped seeing it as a debt.

For some, it has become a sort of thirteenth salary.

For others, it is the cushion that allows them to survive the final days of the month.

And for many, without realizing it, it ended up becoming a permanent life companion.

The problem is that permanent companions are usually expensive.

---

## The Record Almost Nobody Talks About

According to data from the **Central Bank of Kosovo (BQK)**, citizens accumulate more than **500 million euros** in overdraft lines and the allowed minus.

More than half a billion euros borrowed to cover daily expenses.

We are not talking about loans to open businesses.

We are not talking about real estate investments.

In many cases, we are talking about gasoline.

About supermarkets.

About medicines.

About the electricity bill.

Because when private sector salaries frequently hover between 500 and 700 euros monthly, while the cost of living continues to rise, many families do not enter the minus to live better.

They enter simply to reach day thirty.

---

## The Difference Between a Loan and a Treadmill

A traditional credit line has something very important: a beginning and an end.

You ask for money.

You pay a fee.

Every month you owe a little less.

Until one day the debt disappears.

The allowed minus works differently.

It looks much more like a gym treadmill.

You run.

You make an effort.

Your legs sweat.

But when you look up, you discover that you are exactly in the same spot.

Imagine this situation:

You use 1,000 euros of the minus.

Your salary arrives.

The bank automatically recovers the money.

Your account breathes for two or three days.

Then you use the minus again to fill the car's tank, buy food, or pay an unexpected bill.

And then the cycle begins again.

Month after month.

Year after year.

You are not amortizing the debt.

You are paying for the right to keep having debt.

---

## The Math of the Drip

Here appears the part that almost no one perceives.

The interests on the minus are usually calculated daily.

They don't make noise.

They don't arrive as a huge bill in the mailbox.

They are small drops constantly falling on the same stone.

One euro here.

Two euros there.

One less coffee this week.

Another less coffee the next.

The problem is that drops also fill pools.

At the end of the year, many people discover they have paid dozens or even hundreds of euros simply to keep the door to borrowed money open.

They bought nothing new with that money.

They did not significantly reduce the debt.

They simply paid rent to use for a few days the salary they had not yet earned.

---

## The Minus is Not the Enemy; the Enemy is Living Inside It

It would be unfair to demonize this tool.

Emergencies exist.

Hospitals do not wait.

Cars break down precisely when one can least afford it.

Children need things exactly when the budget is tightest.

The problem begins when the spare tire becomes the main tire.

Because nobody drives calmly for years using only the emergency wheel.

---

## How to Start Climbing Out of the Circle

The exit rarely happens all at once.

Usually, it happens in centimeters.

If the bank authorized a minus of 1,200 euros, try to reduce that limit little by little.

First to 1,000.

Then to 800.

Then to 600.

Each reduction is a small invisible victory.

There is also an even more important mental exercise:

If every month your salary automatically disappears to cover the minus, then your real salary is not the one appearing in the contract.

Your real salary is what remains after paying that debt.

Accepting that figure hurts.

But it is also usually the first step to regain control.

---

## The Most Expensive Comfort of the Financial System

The allowed minus was designed to be comfortable.

It requires no paperwork.

It requires no visits to the bank.

It requires no explanations.

It's there.

Silent.

Available.

Waiting.

And precisely because of that, it can be so dangerous.

Because the debts that scare us the most are usually the ones we see.

The ones that come with visible installments.

The ones accompanied by deadlines and reminders.

The minus, on the other hand, has the elegance of disappearing before you even have time to notice it.

It captures the salary before you can even touch it.

Therefore, sorting out personal finances does not always start by earning more money.

Sometimes it starts by doing something much more difficult: stop paying rent for the money you haven't yet gone out to earn with your own hard effort.
