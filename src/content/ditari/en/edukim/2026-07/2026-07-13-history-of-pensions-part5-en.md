In the previous four articles, we covered the entire foundational journey. We discovered why pensions were originally invented, understood how the pay-as-you-go system operates, analyzed individual capitalization, and examined why advanced economies combine different pillars to diversify risk.

Now, with all the pieces of the financial clock disassembled on the table, we can ask the concrete question: What happens when a society relies almost exclusively on a state-backed pay-as-you-go rope, but fewer and fewer working-age people are left to hold it up?

That is precisely the systemic challenge confronting Albania today.

---

## The Myth of Institutional Trust: Public vs. Private?

Before diving into Albania's internal metrics, we must address an uncomfortable question.

Imagine you have saved €10,000 through years of hard work. You do not want to store it under a mattress where inflation will slowly erode its purchasing power; you want that capital to grow. However, you do not possess the skills to navigate financial markets yourself. You decide to hand that money over to someone else to manage. You have two options.

The first person is your cousin. He studied economics at a top-tier university, has experience in corporate finance, understands capital markets, and explains his investment strategies clearly. The second person is also your cousin, but he is a drug addict dealing with severe personal issues who has a proven track record of making disastrous financial choices—someone capable of stealing from his own mother to feed his addiction.

The obvious answer is that you would trust the first one. But the real macroeconomic question is: **what happens if even the first cousin makes a mistake?**

That is the raw reality of any retirement system. It does not matter whether the funds are managed by a public institution or a private corporation; the risk of error, asset mismanagement, or systemic inefficiency always exists. A state-run institution can be poorly managed, bogged down by bureaucracy, or driven by short-term political interests.

On the flip side, a private financial institution is not synonymous with absolute infallibility either. It may possess spectacular offices in downtown Tirana or Pristina, a modern corporate headquarters, executives dressed in expensive tailored suits, and flawless marketing campaigns. Yet, none of that guarantees future returns. A glass door in a luxury skyscraper is not mathematical proof that someone knows how to safely multiply your lifetime savings.

The correct question developed societies ask is not who looks more reliable at first glance, but **what strict rules and oversight structures exist to prevent bad management from destroying the future of millions of citizens?** Resilient systems rely on independent supervision, transparent audits, and state-backed insurance funds. This is the exact regulatory benchmark we must apply to Albania.

## The ISSH and the Breakdown of the Albanian Pay-As-You-Go Rope

The system managed by the *Instituti i Sigurimeve Shoqërore* (ISSH) belongs to the pure pay-as-you-go model (the red rope). Active workers of today directly fund the pensions of current retirees through mandatory social security contributions.

During the late 20th century, this model was highly sustainable because the Albanian demographic pyramid was young and broad at its base. There was a large volume of active contributors injecting liquidity into the general pool and relatively few beneficiaries withdrawing from it. The safety line was exceptionally robust.

Today, that internal mathematical equation has broken down. Albania faces a perfect storm where the state safety line is losing its anchors at an alarming speed due to a primary factor: **demographic hemorrhage** (in other words, people emigrating on a massive scale).

Over the past few decades, the country has recorded one of the highest emigration rates in Europe. Hundreds of thousands of citizens have left the country. For the state pension system, the disaster is not just the net loss of population on the census records, but that the vast majority of those who emigrate are young adults in their peak productive and reproductive years. By moving to Germany, Italy, or the United Kingdom, Albania does not just lose human capital; it permanently loses the social security contributions required to fund the current system.

---

## The Two Fronts of Albanian Fiscal Strain

The human bucket brigade we described in Part 2 is running out of hands to support the weight. The structural strain on the fiscal accounts of the ISSH is hitting both ends of the system simultaneously:

*   **Shrinking Revenue Base:** The collapse of the local fertility rate (hovering around 1.2 children per woman) paired with youth emigration severely compresses the pool of formal tax-paying workers.
*   **Expanding Expenditure Ceiling:** Thanks to extended life expectancies, current retirees draw their pension benefits for significantly more years.

The dependency ratio has deteriorated to critical levels, sitting near one formal worker for every single retiree. International experts estimate that a pure pay-as-you-go model requires a minimum of three active contributors per beneficiary to remain balanced without accumulating massive public debt.

---

## The Informal Economy and the Grey Wage

The second major structural hurdle within the Albanian economy is informal employment and under-declaration of income (the widespread phenomenon of declaring the legal minimum wage while delivering the remainder of the actual salary under the table in cash).

A worker operating today without registering at the ISSH or declaring a fictional wage base may feel they have more immediate liquid cash in their pocket. However, what they are actually doing is shifting a major financial crisis into their own future. Upon reaching retirement age, their accumulated contributions will be mathematically insignificant, pushing them into poverty or forcing the state to bail them out through non-contributory social pensions funded by general public taxes.

This is not a localized dilemma for today's elderly. It is a silent macroeconomic crisis affecting active workers currently aged 30, 40, or 50. The crucial question we must ask is not just whether the ISSH can clear next month's pension run; the real question is **what asset baseline will be left when today's working class reaches retirement age?**

---

## The Future Challenge: Designing a Multi-Pillar System

Albania will not see its state pension system vanish overnight, because the state budget will always step in to cover the financial deficit of the ISSH using revenue gathered from general taxation. The true risk is not a technical default, but rather **chronic insufficiency**: pension payouts so low that they force the elderly to rely entirely on family remittances or to continue working informally well into old age.

Mature economic discussion in Albania should move past simplistic, ideological debates of *"State vs. Private sector"*. The right path forward involves designing a smart transition toward a mixed or multi-pillar system, where the public, solidarity-based tier of the ISSH is insulated by strict regulatory controls, but structurally supplemented by mandatory personal long-term savings and investment mechanisms.

The demographic mountain will not halt its advance. The current generation of workers will inevitably age, and Albania's long-term economic success depends on whether we are capable of braiding a resilient new safety line, or if we choose to remain suspended from a crumbling structure where fewer and fewer hands are left to support the weight of everyone, and the red rope is getting weaker and weaker.
