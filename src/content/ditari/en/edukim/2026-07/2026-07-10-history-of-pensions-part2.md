In the previous article, we saw why pensions were born.

People grow old. There comes a time when they can no longer work. And society decided to create a system so that no one would be left completely destitute.

But one pending question remained: Where does that money come from every single month?

Many people imagine something like this: *"Throughout my entire working life, I saved money in a sort of safe deposit box. When I retire, they will simply return that exact same money to me."*

It sounds logical. But in much of the world, it does not work that way.

---

## The Pay-As-You-Go System: A Human Chain

Let's think of a very simple scene.

A small village suffers a fire. There is a river nearby, but it is far from the flames. So the neighbors form a long line. The first person fills a bucket with water. They hand it to the next person. Then to the next. And so on until it reaches the last neighbor, who puts out the fire.

No one runs the entire distance. Everyone does a small part of the work.

Now imagine that this chain represents an entire society. Young workers are at the beginning. Retirees are at the end.

Every month, the exact same thing happens. Workers contribute a portion of their salary. That money passes through the system. And finally, it reaches those who have already stopped working.

---

## The Money Does Not Wait Forty Years

Here is something that surprises many people.

In this system, the money usually does not spend decades waiting in an account with your name on it. In simple terms, **the money contributed by today's workers is used to pay the pensions of today's retirees.**

It is an agreement between generations. You help the previous generation. With the expectation that, when your turn comes, the next generation will do the same for you.

That is why this model is often called a **pay-as-you-go** or solidarity system. Not because everyone receives exactly the same amount. But because one generation sustains another.

---

## The Baby Boom: When the Chain Was Long

For many years, this model worked quite well. And it was no coincidence.

After World War II, many countries experienced what is known as the *baby boom*. Millions of children were born. Decades later, those children became millions of workers.

There were many people contributing money and relatively few retirees. The human chain was long and strong. The bucket reached the end without any problems.

---

## Demographic Collapse: What Happens When the Chain Breaks?

Now let's imagine the exact same scene. But this time, something different happens.

Some people emigrate. Others have fewer children. The population ages. There are increasingly more retirees. And increasingly fewer workers.

The chain still exists. But now it has fewer people holding it together.

The result is obvious. Every active worker must sustain a larger share of the system. Not because they are working less. But because there are fewer people sharing the heavy lifting.

---

## The Dependency Ratio: A Family Example

Imagine a family where four siblings financially support their elderly parents.

Each contributes 100 euros. Together, they gather 400 euros.

Now imagine that two siblings move to another country. The parents still need the same 400 euros to live. But now only two people are left contributing at home.

Each would have to give 200 euros to maintain the exact same level of support. The problem did not appear because the parents are spending more. It appeared because there are fewer people contributing.

The exact same thing happens, on a macroeconomic scale, in a pension system when the workforce shrinks.

By the way, if you ever hear an economist on television using a highly convoluted expression like **"demographic dependency ratio"**, do not panic. Experts love to use complicated terms to show off and sound like they know more than you do.

But now you know exactly what it means. It is just the fancy way of saying: *"how many siblings are left at home to help carry the water bucket."*

---

## So... Is the System Doomed?

Not necessarily.

Pay-as-you-go systems have worked for decades and continue to function in many countries. But they depend on a very delicate mathematical and demographic balance:

*   How many people are working.
*   How many people are retired.
*   How long the population lives.
*   How much money workers contribute.

When those variables change, the system also needs to adapt. And that is where the reforms (raising the retirement age, increasing taxes) that spark so many debates in different countries begin.

But there is a completely different way to do it.

So far, we have talked about a model where generations help each other. However, some countries followed a different path. They asked themselves a very simple question: **What if, instead of depending on future workers, each person saved for their own retirement?**

That idea gave birth to the individual capitalization system. And, as we will see in the next article, it has fascinating advantages... and very significant risks.
