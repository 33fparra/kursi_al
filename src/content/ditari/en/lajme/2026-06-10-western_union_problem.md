Imagine this situation.  
Your sister lives in Milan. She works from Monday to Saturday, cleans houses, and saves every euro she can. Every month she sends you €300 to help with rent and groceries. She does it with all her heart, with sacrifice, with the promise that the family will be fine.  
But out of those €300... you only get €262.  
The other €38 didn't get lost along the way. Western Union kept them.  
Silently. Without asking for your permission. Without anyone ever explaining it to you.  

In one year, that’s €456 your family never saw. In five years, it’s over €2,000. Real money earned with hard work and sweat, which could have paid off debts, a medical emergency, or even that dental checkup your parents have been putting off for a long time. Or simply, money that should have reached the person who needed it in full.  
And this isn't a rare case. It's what happens to one out of every four Albanian families.  

Albania received over 1 billion euros in remittances last year. Mainly from Italy, Greece, and the United States. That money pays rents, covers bills, pays for school. It is the silent engine of thousands of homes.  
But nobody talks about what gets lost along the way.  
Because the problem isn't that people don't want to send money. The problem is that nobody ever explained to them that Western Union isn't the only option. And that it is, by far, one of the most expensive.

The difference between the worst and the best option: almost €70 per transfer. If you send money once a month, that’s over €800 a year.  

Let's be honest from the start.  
No transfer service works for charity. Everyone profits from your transfer. And that’s normal — it’s a legitimate business that offers a real service.  
The question is not who profits. The question is *how much* they profit, and if it is proportional to the service they provide.  

They all apply the same formula: a visible fee that you see before confirming the transfer, plus an exchange rate margin that almost nobody notices. The difference between the real market exchange rate and the one they apply to you — that difference does not disappear. It is part of their profit.  

**The real exchange rate today officially according to the Bank of Albania is around: 1 euro = 94.9 lekë.** What an expensive service applies to you: 1 euro = 88 lekë.  
What a good competitor applies to you: 1 euro = 94 lekë.  
Same effort. Same destination. A completely different result for your family.  

This is legal. It is transparent if you know where to look. And it exists for a very simple reason: there is competition. Some services are more aggressive with their margin because they know many customers don't compare. Others offer cheaper prices precisely to win over customers who do compare.  

Your responsibility as the person sending money is simple: always compare how much actually arrives at the destination. Not how much you pay. But how much your family receives.  
And one more thing — before looking at the price, look at the reputation. An unknown service that promises the best rate in the world but has only been open for three months is not worth the risk. The services mentioned in this article have been in the market for years, are regulated by law, and have millions of users. Within this trusted group, then yes — choose the most economical one.  

## Which service is the smartest for you?

There is no perfect service for everyone. But there is a smartest choice for every situation.  

* **If you send money regularly and have time to plan it:** Paysend is currently the most competitive option for the Italy-Albania corridor. It has a fixed fee of €1.50 per transfer and an exchange rate very close to the real one.  
* **If your family needs the money urgently today:** Remitly offers delivery speeds in a few minutes and works directly with Albanian bank accounts. The fee is slightly higher than Paysend, but it remains much lower than banks or traditional physical services.  
* **If the person receiving it does not have a bank account:** MoneyGram or Ria have cash pickup locations in almost every city in Albania.  
* **If someone tells you to use a traditional bank for "more security":** The money arrives just as safely with the services above. The only difference is that the bank takes three to five times more money from you for the exact same result.

### ⚠️ BUT BEWARE: The Silent Risk of Digital Apps (Don't Get Blocked Without Warning)

Before you go and download any app or digital wallet (fintech) you see on the internet, there is one very important thing you need to know to protect your family's money.

These new digital companies are great for saving on fees, but they are regulated by extremely strict European laws and operate through **automated security algorithms**. What does this mean for you?

- **Freezes without warning:** If their system sniffs out even the slightest thing it doesn't like (for example: a poorly clear ID document, too frequent transfers within the same day, or sudden increases in amounts), **they will freeze your account immediately**, without any prior warning.
- **Money in "limbo":** When the account is blocked, your money remains trapped inside the app. Customer support in these companies is often slow and responds with automated robotic messages. 
- **No offices in Tirana:** Do not forget that these digital apps do not have a physical office in Tirana or Pristina where you can go in person, knock on the door, and solve the problem with a human face-to-face.

> **The golden rule:** Use these digital apps (like Paysend, Remitly, or Paysera) only as a **"bridge"** to transfer money directly to your Albanian bank account or to withdraw it as cash at authorized locations. But never keep your savings blocked and deposited inside the app for a long time.
