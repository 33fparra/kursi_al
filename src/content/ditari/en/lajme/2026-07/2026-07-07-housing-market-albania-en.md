A few days ago, I spoke with a Japanese friend who lives in Tirana.

If a manual for the \"perfect tenant\" existed, he would probably be the ideal example. He is extremely clean. He looks after the apartment as if it were his own. He is responsible with payments. He respects the building rules, and you will rarely find a person who treats a home with more care than he does. The exact type of person any landlord should want to keep for years.

Yet, he is still being forced to look for another place to live.

Not because he stopped paying. Not because he caused problems. Not because he deteriorated the apartment.

The reason is much simpler and, at the same time, far more complex: his home can generate more money in another way.

And here an uncomfortable question surfaces: if you were the landlord, what would you do?

Imagine you own an apartment in Tirana. For years, you rent it to a responsible person for 500 euros a month. Everything works perfectly. But then you discover that similar apartments in your area are generating 700, 800, or even more euros through short-term holiday rentals.

You have nothing against your tenant. You do not want to harm them. But you think: *"It is my property. Why should I earn less than other owners?"*

That individual decision, repeated by thousands of people at the exact same time, is transforming Albania's real estate market.

---

## When a Property Stops Being Just a Home

Barely ten years ago, when someone bought an apartment in Tirana or Sarandë, they usually had two options: live there or rent it out all year round to a family.

Today, a third alternative has completely changed the rules of the game: converting that apartment into a micro-hotel.

No new building needs to be constructed. There is no need to set up a traditional hospitality corporate structure. Often, all it takes is a few good photographs, a mobile app, and a digital platform like Airbnb or Booking.

The problem appears when thousands of homes originally intended for permanent residents begin to compete in the same market as tourist accommodation. Because a property that was previously available for someone who lives, works, and pays taxes in the city can now be allocated to hosting visitors for just a few days.

Tourism grows. But the volume of housing units available for those who actually live there shrinks drastically.

The individual landlord is not necessarily acting out of malice toward local families. They are simply responding directly to the massive economic incentive that the market itself has generated. Who is to blame? No single decision is malicious. The real issue surfaces when thousands of individual rational decisions compile into a collective structural problem.

## Why Do Rents Climb After the Summer?

It is logical for prices to increase during the summer. It happens in any tourist destination: hotels are more expensive in peak season, restaurants have more customers, and accommodations raise their prices when demand grows. That is how the market functions.

The real problem appears when the season ends. In theory, when the volume of tourists drops, prices should return approximately to their previous levels. But in practice, that rarely happens.

The price spikes during the summer, and when winter arrives, it edges down slightly but remains firmly anchored near the new peak. Why? Because the temporary high season rate starts to become the new baseline reference for the neighborhood.

If an owner notes that a similar apartment rented for significantly more money a few months prior, they will try to preserve that valuation. There is no need for a conspiracy or a secret pact among landlords. Each person simply makes a decision trying to optimize their financial position, but the collective result is a permanent increase in residential housing costs.

---

## Will Building More Apartments Fix the Rental Market?

A common, automated response to the issue is: *"Then we need to build more housing."* In textbook economic theory, it seems logical: more apartments should mean more supply and more balanced prices.

But the truly important question is: **More housing for whom?**

If a dominant percentage of new apartments are built as speculative investments, holiday rentals, or empty properties waiting for land values to appreciate, the volume of buildings in Tirana will expand, the skyline will entirely transform, but the real supply floor for those who need to live there will remain suffocatingly tight.

It doesn’t just matter how many physical roofs exist across a zip code. It matters how many of those roofs are genuinely available for the permanent residents of the city.

---

## Squeezing the Balance Between Tourism and Real Life

The growth of tourism in Albania is an undeniable, historic opportunity. It creates essential employment, attracts strong foreign capital inflows, helps small local family businesses, and channels new money into the economic circuit. Viewing tourism as the enemy would be a major strategic error.

But ignoring its collateral damage would be an equally severe mistake. A prosperous city needs visitors who spend money, but it also structurally relies on the individuals who keep the city running every single day. It needs teachers, doctors, nurses, police officers, and young students who can afford to gain independence from their parents.

A city where the essential workforce can no longer afford to live within its borders faces a severe long-term structural issue. Albania's challenge is not choosing between tourism or residential housing; the challenge lies in designing smart frameworks so that economic growth does not end up displacing the exact people who keep the city alive.
