Every afternoon or evening, a similar scene plays out in thousands of households across Albania: a WhatsApp video call rings. On one side of the screen is a living room in Tirana, Shkodër, or Durrës. On the other, a small, cold rented room in Germany or Italy. This is the true face of migration today. And this crisis is not measured just by numbers; there are several human and economic factors we must take into account.

Many people still think that leaving the country is like it was in the '90s, when you set off with just a backpack and not a single penny in your pocket. Today, in the year 2026, things have completely changed. Sending a child abroad has turned into a luxury project. To export a bright mind, Albanian families must pay a heavy upfront "tax," draining a lifetime of savings before the plane even takes off from Tirana.

---

## How Much Exactly Does the "Escape Package" Cost?

To send a young person from Albania to a university or a vocational training program (like the *Ausbildung* in Germany), parents must face harsh mathematics before the bags are even packed:

* **Endless language courses:** Two or three years of paying for private German or English lessons, plus the steep fees for official examinations.
* **Paperwork and bureaucracy:** Official translations, notarizations, apostilles, and consular fees.
* **The Blocked Account (Sperrkonto):** This is the hardest blow. To grant a student visa, the German embassy requires an immediate deposit of over **12,300 euros** into a blocked foreign bank account, simply to prove the student can survive there.

If you add the plane ticket and the first few months of rent to this figure, the "Escape Package" costs an Albanian family at least **15,000 euros in cash**. This is net capital leaving local shops and the Albanian economy, transferred directly into foreign banks.

## The Worst Business for Our Country

From a financial perspective, this exodus is the worst possible deal for Albania. Why? Because it operates as a double-edged sword:

1. **Parents sending money out:** Instead of emigrants sending remittances back home to stimulate the domestic market, it is now parents inside Albania sending money to their children in Munich or Milan just to keep them afloat.
2. **A ready-made gift for Europe:** Albania pays to vaccinate, feed, and educate these children in public schools for 18 consecutive years. Yet, at the exact moment they are ready to work, consume, and pay taxes, we hand them over to Germany or Italy completely free of charge.

This is precisely why in 2026 local businesses, hospitals, and tech hubs are suffering like never before. It doesn't matter how many new skyscrapers are erected; we are running out of the very people who should be working inside them.

## Unfiltered: A Decision Made of Love, a Crisis for Everyone

Let's be honest: no one can blame a father or a mother who empties their pockets to send their child abroad. When you look around and see that the local market still suffers from low wages, cronyism, corruption, and a reliance on connections, wanting to offer your child a secure future is completely logical. It is an act of pure love. Personally, I wouldn't want my child to grow up in a place where meritocracy is increasingly disappearing, and therefore their opportunities diminish even when they are exceptionally good at what they do.

But we must also face the other side of the coin: a country that fills up with new walls but empties itself of its youth cannot have a sustainable future. True development isn't measured by the tons of cement poured each year, but by the opportunities we build right here on our own pavements, so our youth can see that staying in Albania is a better business than leaving it.
